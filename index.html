Title
When to Use Machine Learning vs Deep Learning: A Real-World Comparative Analysis
Introduction
Machine Learning and Deep Learning are often used interchangeably, but they are distinct tools with different strengths. Choosing the wrong approach for a given problem leads to unnecessary computational cost, poor accuracy, or both. This artifact explores two real-world examples — housing price prediction and image recognition — to illustrate when each paradigm is the right choice and, equally importantly, when it is not.
Description
The artifact presents a side-by-side comparison of two production-grade applications of AI: a classical ML use case (linear regression for housing prices) and a deep learning use case (CNNs for autonomous vehicle perception). For each, the analysis covers the real-world deployment context, justification for the chosen approach, and a reasoned argument for why the alternative paradigm would be unsuitable.
  • Example 1 — Housing Price Prediction (ML / Linear Regression): Real estate platforms like Zillow estimate home values using features such as square footage, number of bedrooms, property age, and location. Linear regression models each feature's contribution to price as a linear relationship — e.g., larger square footage → higher price. Deep learning is unsuitable here because the dataset is structured and numerical, relationships are linear and interpretable, and adding a neural network adds computational cost without meaningful accuracy gain.
  • Example 2 — Image Recognition (Deep Learning / CNN): Autonomous vehicle systems at companies like Tesla and Waymo use Convolutional Neural Networks to identify road signs, pedestrians, obstructions, and other vehicles in real time from onboard camera feeds. CNNs excel here because images contain millions of pixels with complex spatial patterns (edges, textures, shapes) that CNNs can detect regardless of position in the frame. Traditional ML algorithms like logistic regression or SVMs are unsuitable because they cannot scale to the volume and dimensionality of image data, require manual feature engineering that becomes infeasible for unstructured data, and cannot automatically extract hierarchical visual features.
Objective
  • Understand the practical distinction between classical Machine Learning and Deep Learning in applied settings
  • Identify the characteristics of a problem — data type, volume, structure, and relationships — that determine which approach is appropriate
  • Demonstrate that model selection is driven by problem constraints, not by recency or complexity of the technique
  • Build intuition for evaluating trade-offs between model simplicity, interpretability, computational cost, and accuracy
Process
  • Problem Selection — Identified two well-known, production-deployed AI systems representing opposite ends of the ML/DL spectrum
  • Literature & Industry Research — Reviewed how Zillow's Zestimate and Tesla/Waymo's perception stacks work at a high level
  • Comparative Framework — Structured the analysis around three questions for each example: What is the real-world use case? Why is this approach suitable? Why is the alternative not suitable?
  • Write-up — Synthesized findings into clear, non-jargon explanations grounded in the technical properties of each model class
Tools and Technologies Used
  • Linear Regression — Classical ML algorithm; models the linear relationship between numerical input features and a continuous output variable (price)
  • Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) — Deep learning architecture designed for spatial data; uses convolutional layers to detect local patterns such as edges and textures across image grids
  • Zillow Zestimate — Reference production system illustrating ML applied to real estate valuation
  • Tesla Autopilot / Waymo — Reference production systems illustrating CNN-based real-time object detection for autonomous driving
Value Proposition
Unique Value

Rather than describing ML and deep learning in the abstract, this artifact grounds the comparison in two systems that millions of people interact with daily. This makes the trade-offs — interpretability, compute cost, data requirements, feature engineering burden — concrete and memorable rather than theoretical.

Relevance

Model selection is one of the most consequential decisions in any ML project. This artifact directly builds the judgment needed to make that call: understanding why linear regression is the right tool for structured numerical data, and why CNNs are necessary for high-dimensional unstructured data, is foundational knowledge applicable across virtually every domain in the course.

Finished Product

A neural network is a way for a machine to learn patterns from experience, similar to how a human learns by practicing and improving over time. Think of it as a simple three-part system:

Input
The information you give it — a picture, a number, a sentence.
Hidden Layers
Tiny decision-makers asking: “Does it have ears? Is it furry? Are the eyes big?”
Output
The final answer — “this is a cat” or “this is not a cat.”

Think of it like a factory: raw material goes in, gets processed, and a finished product comes out.

The learning process happens in small repeated steps:

1. Guess → 2. Check → 3. Adjust → 4. Repeat

Like a game: wrong guesses get small penalties, correct answers get rewards, and each round helps it improve. Over time, it becomes very good at the task.

The “thinking” part is made up of tiny units called neurons. Each neuron gives a small signal or vote, and stronger signals matter more than weaker ones. It’s like asking a group of friends to decide something — if most say “cat” and only a few say “dog,” the final answer is “cat.”

Three things to remember:

  • 1.It learns from examples
  • 2.Input → Process → Output
  • 3.Guess → Check → Adjust → Repeat

A real-life example: face recognition on your phone. The system learned your face from many examples and now compares new images to decide if it’s really you.

In one sentence: a neural network is a system that learns patterns from examples and gets better by practicing again and again.

Finished Product

Fraud Detection in Imbalanced and Evolving Data: Research Insights

Fraud detection is a classic challenge in machine learning due to two major issues: extreme class imbalance — fraudulent transactions are rare — and temporal concept drift, where fraud patterns evolve over time. Through research, I explored strategies for designing reliable, business-aligned fraud detection systems under these conditions.

Key Insights

1. Data Preparation

Maintaining chronological order is critical to respect temporal drift. Feature engineering that captures behavior over time — such as rolling averages or transaction deltas — helps models detect subtle anomalies. Class imbalance can be effectively addressed using class-weighted loss, with decision thresholds tuned based on the expected cost of missed fraud versus false alarms.

2. Modeling Approaches

Supervised classification models (e.g., gradient boosting) are effective for known fraud patterns, while anomaly detection can supplement detection of previously unseen fraudulent behavior. Rolling-window training and incremental updates allow models to remain adaptive as patterns change.

3. Evaluation & Threshold Selection

Time-aware validation (walk-forward or rolling splits) is essential to simulate deployment. Metrics like precision, recall, PR-AUC, and expected financial cost provide a more realistic picture than accuracy alone. Thresholds should be chosen using both these metrics and business inputs: the cost of missed fraud, cost of false alarms, operational capacity, and risk appetite.

4. Production Considerations

Continuous monitoring of model performance, prediction distributions, and data drift ensures the system remains effective over time. Retraining can be scheduled periodically or triggered by performance degradation, and a champion–challenger setup helps safely deploy updated models.

This research highlights how fraud detection requires balancing technical modeling with business impact, and emphasizes the importance of adaptive, time-aware strategies in real-world systems.

Amazon’s AI Recruiting Tool: A Case Study in Algorithmic Bias and the Risks of Unchecked Machine Learning
Introduction
As AI systems are increasingly deployed in high-stakes decision-making contexts — hiring, lending, healthcare, criminal justice — the question of fairness and accountability becomes critical. This artifact examines a real AI incident that exemplifies what can go wrong when machine learning models are trained on historically biased data and deployed without sufficient oversight. The Amazon recruiting tool incident of 2018 is a landmark case that exposes how technically sophisticated systems can produce discriminatory outcomes at scale, and what obligations developers have to prevent this.
Description

The model had been trained on a decade of historical hiring data in which the tech workforce was overwhelmingly male. As a result, the model learned to associate maleness with hiring success, effectively encoding gender discrimination into its scoring logic. Amazon scrapped the system after internal audits revealed the bias. The incident triggered widespread debate about AI fairness, transparency, and accountability in automated decision-making. Finished Product

Incident Summary

In 2018, it was discovered that the recruiting tool developed by Amazon to automate the screening of resumes systematically penalized resumes containing indicators of a candidate being female. Since the tool was trained on historical hiring data where the tech force was dominated by male candidates, the model learned and got ingrained with a bias to favor male candidates. Amazon eventually scrapped the system, but this incident raised awareness and concerns about fairness, accountability, and the risks associated with deploying AI models for important decision-making. This incident also exposes how machine learning models can exacerbate and reinforce existing biases if not trained with the right data.

Critical Analysis

1. Bias and Discrimination Caused by the Algorithm

Why: The bias of the algorithm directly disadvantaged a protected group and went against the concept of equal employment opportunities.

Implications: The model learned from biased historical data and propagated the bias rather than learning what objective qualifications the hired candidates had. This revealed how AI systems are never neutral — they always amplify existing inequalities. This is serious because decisions like hiring affect livelihoods and long-term career opportunities, transforming a technical glitch into a systemic fairness concern.

Solution: Implementing fairness constraints and checks during and after model training can alleviate these issues. Training with data that inherently reflects diversity would reduce the recurrence of these discriminatory outcomes.

2. Lack of Transparency

Why: It wasn’t immediately apparent that there was a bias in the system, highlighting how opaque these AI systems can be.

Implications: With recruiters relying on these systems, the bias would not have been apparent. They had no understanding of how decisions were made, and as a result, could not challenge unfair outcomes. It also makes accountability vague — nobody can really explain the decisions that were taken.

Solution: Make documentation of model behavior mandatory. This would enable stakeholders to understand the decisions and question them if necessary.

3. Overreliance on Historical Data

Why: The root cause of this issue is trusting historical data blindly without accounting for the bias it might contain, and assuming it will reliably predict future outcomes.

Implications: Historical data can reflect societal biases rather than objective truth. Training on this data makes AI models accentuate and propagate these biases — an underlying ethical issue, since data is never neutral or free of bias.

Solution: Use debiasing strategies at the model training, post-processing, and continuous monitoring phases of the model lifecycle — from using representative data to conducting fairness audits during ongoing monitoring.

Reflection

I initially assumed that AI systems perform poorly mainly because of model design or implementation issues. Even though the smartest minds at Amazon would have worked on making the model itself as technically sound as possible, the AI ended up being biased purely because of the data itself. This shows that even technically excellent products can perform poorly if data quality suffers. It doesn’t matter how much data one has access to — it’s the quality of the training data that matters most.

This analysis will influence my future approach to building AI models. I would prioritize data quality above all, and incorporate fairness checks and ethical review processes throughout the model development lifecycle. I would ensure datasets are examined thoroughly and models are continuously audited to prevent unintended biases.
